The field of analytics has always been a staple of industry. Early versions of this were embodied as ad hoc reports in which records were presented in both spreadsheet format with oral presentation and later on as graphical representations. These reports were often viewed with the intent of adjusting business strategy in more of a retrospective view. In the pharmaceutical industry, this can mean always being a step behind. 



The growth of predictive and prescriptive analytics has hinged on the availability of new technology to become easily accessible. The pharmaceutical industry is no different. The growth and reduction of cost of for big data technologies has allowed for advances across several industries. To put it as simple as possible, without data technologies, any system will be left in the past. 



One unique value that is available in the pharmaceutical realm in data is the understanding of the side effects of a new medication. Obviously, there is always a need for reporting and documentation of unexpected impacts of a new medication. However, with the use of machine learning and the right data source, you can accurately predict and stay ahead of potential pitfalls with the medication. 



Decreasing the risk exposure through the use of analytics is not the only benefit to implantation. The largest benefit more so rests on the change in brand and the change in potential markets. 


The brand change is somewhat of a newer and hidden benefit. In most situations, the company’s brand image dictates where they fall in line in terms of views by the consumer.  By utilizing machine learning techniques, you can better suit the brand to be more personalized to the customer. 


This leads us to the concept of utilizing analytics in the biosphere to better and more accurately predict the next market for a medication. One particular example of this is using things like logistic regression and clustering to predict the next large-scale need for a particular medication. By studying the disease patterns and processes, a company can leverage these techniques to produce and make readily available medications before it becomes a large-scale event. 



The best example of this is the AIDS epidemic in Africa. Approaching this from a pharmaceutical point of view, there are a hundred moving parts on how to best approach both the business and social problem of the AIDS epidemic. This is where by utilizing both analytics and a keen eye for logistics in general, you can stay ahead of the curve. 


As with any manufacturing or distribution system, there will always be a lag time between recognizing a need for a product and the subsequent delivery of the product into the consumers hands. This is where the predictive algorithms within analytics can make the difference between staying ahead of the competition and being the second in to a market. Even if as a company, there is no immediate movement of products, this still gives a window to tackle the potential legal and regulatory challenges.


This also doesn’t absolutely need to rest on customer categorization to be efficient. This equation can also be flipped to encompass the disease process. This would be extremely effective if you are able to quantify the contributing factors to the spread of HIV in certain countries or regions. Recently, this has been done with some success in predicting the spread of the Ebola outbreak in western Africa. By understanding and building a model to represent all of the factors that goes into how a disease is transmitted and how it progresses from incubation to full blown disease, you can accurately predict with some level of accuracy where the disease is likely to be headed and where best to intercept it at.


This doesn’t even encompass the concept of getting the product into these regions. This likely would be a similar process but less reliant on classification and more predictive modeling and forecasting. 


This also relates to the changing of how your company brand is received. By approaching challenges and potential new markets by making your brand customer centric, it makes the company brand much more personable and need focused. A company with the perceived brand reputation of personalizing their product for consumers goes a long way in obtaining and retaining their customer base.


The value of analytics as a whole cannot be overstated, let alone the value within the pharmaceutical industry. The ability to predict and cluster potential side effects within the R&D pipeline would cut costs drastically. In some cases, even utilizing the same technology to select candidate molecules for further research. This would eliminate the extra cost associated with dead end research and development. This would also bring a massive value to the potential for new markets and branding. By understanding what the underlying market forces are and accurately predicting their responses, a company can be both customer centric as well as ahead of all of the competitors.
