This post is a little bit different in the respect that its not going to be as straight forward informational as some the others. One of the things that always seemed to peak my interest is taking a deep dive into a company and seeing how things tick/work/move within that organization. As strange as it may sound, I personally have always found it easier to think of business and entities as a fully functional organism. 

Now that may seem about as strange as it could possibly get but stick with me here and I will explain. My previous life was centered on healthcare and the delivery of said healthcare. I came to understand that you can have someone present perfectly healthy at the first presentation but end up finding out they have a 90% blockage of a coronary artery which is going to kill them. Some of the sickest patients I ever treated initially presented as non-emergent. 

Using this analogy, lets look at a company. Similarly to the human body, a company needs certain vital functions to keep moving forward. At the basic principles, the company needs the ability to make money to pay its bills, people and suppliers. This isn’t as far off from how the human body works but I digress. Using these principles of relating basic life functions, it makes it easier to correlate vital life functions to basic business functions. Once you understand those processes, you can (at least on a basic level) evaluate a company for its solvency/profitability. 

I should specify that this isn’t an apples to apples comparison. Unless someone else has an idea that can relate, I don’t know a human function that would easily equate to a merger or acquisition. The others will be explained below.

When taking basic evaluation of a company, often analysts jump right to numbers to tell a tale. Yes, that is where you can pick apart the system and find where the weak points are but it doesn’t tell the entire tale. 

In medicine, one of the things that is often drilled into many practitioners minds is to treat the patient and not the machine. For those who aren’t familiar with the phrase, it simply means to treat the presentation of the patent and not the specific numbers on a monitor. For those not in medicine, that may seem counter intuitive but it does have it’s merits. Medicine is littered with anecdotal stories of providers treating a patient based on a reading on a monitor or a lab value without evaluating the patient as a whole. Quite simply put, don’t evaluate the patient as dead if the monitor says so but the patient is up and talking to you. 

How does this relate to business? The same rough principle can be applied. As I said earlier, a lot of analysts jump right to a Profit & Loss sheet (P/L) or to a consolidated report to evaluate a company. Yes, this gives you a good image but much like the patient analogy above, its only part of the image. Beginning here, you can evaluate the company as a whole. 

Start by looking at how does the business look? What service does it provide? How are customer reviews? What does it’s pricing look like? How many employees and what divisions do they have? Is the service they provide subject to demand changes or is it stable? 

For those who are unsure what I mean about the demand change, think about an outdoor store vs an utility company. The example here is that you expect profitability for the outdoor store to go down in the winter months but to pick back up in the summer. This is a sharp contrast to the electric company which (barring some catastrophic weather event) will maintain roughly the same level of demand all year round. 

How does that relate to the healthcare? Let me explain, as I previously mentioned, we treat the patient based on how they present and do they appear in distress. On the business front, we know the outdoor store will be a loss during the winter months. This means that their quarterly earnings will show a major decrease during certain times. If you look at just the quarterly earnings for the outdoor store for a year, it looks like it is a dying company. 

This cannot be further from the case but it certainly appears that way on paper. As comical as it may seem even Warren Buffet saw a correlation in measuring the health of a business. 

> “Cash ... is to a business as oxygen is to an individual: never thought about when it is present, the only thing in mind when it is absent” – Warren Buffet

I believe we may have gotten off topic a little. So where does all this tie together? 

This all connects very simply at the intersection of evaluating the entire business and understanding that the metrics that are often relied upon are only part of the part of the picture. When you look at investing or are just evaluating a company. Look at the system as a whole. Don’t get sucked into analysts telling you that you should buy or don’t get wrapped up in the hype. Emotional evaluation will either cause you to miss an important data point. Just because a company has a grade A sales team doesn’t always make up for an extremely poor supply chain or research and development department. 

I’ll leave you with this final thought. When you are looking at a company for any purpose. Regardless if it be for investment, employment or in some cases to purchase it. Look beyond the financial statements and cash flows. Look at the build of the company, the leadership, the flow of goods from inception to delivery to the customer. This will tell you more about the health of a company and its industry than any number of technical indicators or gut feelings or analysts will tell you.
